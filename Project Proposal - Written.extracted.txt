1. Introduction and Background
Housing markets are closely tied to macroeconomic conditions. Monetary policy shapes borrowing costs and credit availability, while inflation and employment dynamics influence household income and purchasing expectations. Because affordability is central to housing demand, shifts in these variables materially affect both transaction activity and price dynamics.
Recent U.S. experience illustrates this. Following the COVID-19 pandemic, national house prices rose roughly 47.7% between Q1 2020 and Q1 2024, with several metro areas exceeding 60% gains (Fu, 2024), driven in part by mortgage rates averaging below 3% in 2021 (Dehan, 2024). Beginning in 2022, the Federal Reserve initiated an aggressive tightening cycle, pushing the 30-year fixed rate to nearly 7% by 2023 (Dehan, 2024). Price growth subsequently decelerated sharply, and some metro areas recorded outright declines (FRED, 2026).
These contrasting episodes suggest that housing price sensitivity is not uniform; it shifts across economic environments. This project systematically investigates instability, focusing on whether and how price responses change across distinct macroeconomic regimes.
2. Related Literature Discussion
This project builds on three interconnected strands of research: housing market dynamics, economic regime modeling, and sentiment analysis in economic and financial forecasting.Housing Market Heterogeneity
Price dynamics vary across property types, locations, and buyer groups. Foundational work by Case and Shiller (1989) and Glaeser et al. (2008) demonstrates that distinct housing submarkets exhibit divergent levels of volatility, supply constraints, and sensitivity to macroeconomic shocks. Property size, quality, and neighborhood characteristics all shape how prices respond to changing economic conditions.
Rather than relying on aggregate housing indices, this project focuses on segment-level price behavior, enabling a more granular understanding of how different apartment types respond to shifting macroeconomic environments.Economic Regime Detection
Macroeconomic relationships evolve over time, particularly during periods of monetary tightening, economic expansion, or recession. Models that assume parameter stability risk missing structural shifts that matter for forecasting. Regime-based approaches, most notably the Markov Switching framework introduced by Hamilton (1989), have long been employed to accommodate such time-varying dynamics.
More recently, machine learning methods have offered flexible alternatives for regime identification. Unsupervised clustering of macroeconomic variables allows latent economic states to be inferred directly from the data, without imposing strong parametric assumptions. This project adopts that approach, augmenting standard macroeconomic indicators with monthly sentiment indices constructed using the Loughran-McDonald financial dictionary. By incorporating sentiment alongside realized economic variables within the clustering framework, the identified regimes capture not only prevailing economic conditions but also evolving market expectations.
Sentiment and Narrative Economics
A growing body of evidence suggests that economic expectations and narratives exert an independent influence on market behavior, amplifying cycles and shaping real outcomes beyond what fundamentals alone would predict. In financial markets, text-based sentiment extracted from news sources and policy communications has been shown to predict returns, volatility, and investor responses.
Federal Reserve communications are an example: the hawkish or dovish language can shift interest rate expectations and broader market conditions. Yet relatively little research has examined whether sentiment meaningfully improves housing market forecasts — and still less has explored whether its predictive value is contingent on the prevailing macroeconomic regime. This project directly addresses both questions.
Contribution to the Literature
This study integrates three components that are typically examined in isolation: data-driven macroeconomic regime identification, segment-level housing market analysis, and text-based sentiment measures. Their combination yields a more comprehensive framework for understanding housing price dynamics.
The central empirical contribution is to test whether sentiment enhances predictive performance conditional on the identified macroeconomic regime — thereby contributing to the literature on state-dependent forecasting and the role of economic narratives in real estate markets.
3. Guiding Research Questions
Central Research Question
Do apartment prices respond differently to macroeconomic conditions across economic regimes and apartment segments, and does accounting for these regimes improve prediction accuracy?
Supporting Research Questions
To address the central research question, the analysis proceeds in four sequential stages:
Identification of Economic Regimes: Would Federal Funds Rate, unemployment, PCE inflation, and mortgage spread cluster into distinct macroeconomic regimes?
Changing Price Responses: Does apartment price differ across the identified macroeconomic regimes, and do these differences vary by apartment types?
Value of Recognizing Economic Regimes: Does incorporating regime classification and measures of economic sentiment improve the explanation and prediction of price movements?

4.3 FOMC Text Data
Approximately 80 FOMC Minutes (5,000–9,000 words each) and 80 Policy Statements (400–600 words each) are collected from the Federal Reserve website over 2015–2025 using Python's requests and BeautifulSoup libraries. Each record contains four fields: meeting date, document type (minutes or statement), source URL, and cleaned body text. Sentiment scores are derived using the Loughran-McDonald financial dictionary to produce monthly indices of policy tone (hawkish vs. dovish), capturing evolving market expectations beyond what hard macroeconomic data alone can convey.

5. Data Tidying
5.1 Apartment Transaction Data
Monthly Excel files are programmatically concatenated via a Google Colab pipeline. Rows containing embedded summary statistics (averages, medians, totals) are removed, as are records with missing or invalid address fields. Column names are standardized, sale prices are coerced to numeric, and sale dates are parsed to datetime format. ZIP codes are assigned via the sub-neighborhood mapping described above, yielding a clean, analysis-ready transaction CSV.
5.2 Macroeconomic Data
Four preprocessing steps are applied. First, MORTGAGE30US is resampled from weekly to monthly frequency using within-month averages. Second, PCE is transformed into a year-over-year percentage change (PCE_YOY) to address non-stationarity, and this change is verified using the Augmented Dickey-Fuller test across all series. Third, FEDFUNDS and MORTGAGE30US are combined into MORTGAGE_SPREAD to reduce multicollinearity while retaining the credit signal. Finally, FRED's native missing-value marker (".") is replaced with NaN, and incomplete rows are dropped, yielding a complete monthly panel from January 2015 to December 2025.
5.3 FOMC Text Data
A two-pass cleaning pipeline is applied. During scraping, BeautifulSoup isolates the main content block (div#content) and removes navigation menus, headers, footers, and script elements, ensuring only substantive policy text is retained. A subsequent script handles residual noise: URLs and HTML entities are stripped, lines under 40 characters are dropped as likely interface fragments, boilerplate phrases in Fed are removed via pattern matching, and whitespace is normalized. Any document falling below 100 words post-cleaning is flagged and excluded. The final output file (fomc_cleaned.csv) is ready for sentiment scoring.
